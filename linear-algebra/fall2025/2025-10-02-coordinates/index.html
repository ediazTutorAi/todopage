Coordinates

Definition 3.4.1
Coordinate in a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$
Consider a basis $\mathfrak{B}=(\vec{v}_1,\cdots,\vec{v}_m)$ of a subspace V of $\mathbb{R}^n$. By Theorem 3.2.10, any vector $\vec{x}$ in V can be written uniquely $$\vec{x}=c_1\vec{v}_1+\cdots+c_m\vec{v}_m$$ The scalars $c_1,c_2,\cdots,c_m$ are called the $\mathfrak{B}$-coordinates of $\vec{x}$, and the vector $$\begin{bmatrix}c_1\\c_2\\ \vdots\\c_m\end{bmatrix}$$ is the $\mathfrak{B}$-coordinate vector of $\vec{x}$, denoted by $[\vec{x}]_{\mathfrak{B}}$. Thus, $$[\vec{x}]_{\mathfrak{B}}=\begin{bmatrix}c_1\\c_2\\ \vdots \\c_m\end{bmatrix}$$ means that $\vec{x}=c_1\vec{v}_1+c_2\vec{v}_2+\cdots+c_m\vec{v}_m$
Note that
$$\vec{x}=S[\vec{x}]_{\mathfrak{B}}$$ where $$S=\begin{bmatrix}\end{bmatrix}$$
Theorem 3.4.4
Standard matrix versus $\mathfrak{B}-matrix$
Consider a linear transformation T from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$ and a basis $\mathfrak{B}=(\vec{v_1},\cdots\vec{v_n})$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Let B be the $\mathfrak{B}$-matrix of T, and let A be the standard matrix of T (such that $T(x)=Ax$ for all x in $\mathbb{R}^n$). Then
$$AS=SB,B=S^{-1}AS,A=SBS^{-1}$$
Definition 3.4.5
Similar matrices
Consider two $n \times n$ matrices A and B. We say that A is similar to B if there exists an invertible matrix S such that $$AS=SB, or B=S^{-1}AS$$
Theorem 3.4.6
Similarity is an equivalence relation
a. An $n\times n$ matrix A is similar to A itself
b. If A is similar to B, then B is similar to A
c. If A is similar to B and B is similar to C then A is similar to C
Theorem 3.4.7
When is the \(\mathfrak{B}\)-matrix of T diagonal
Consider a linear transformation \(T(\vec{x})=A \vec{x}\) from \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\) to \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\). Let \(\mathfrak{B}=(\vec{v_1},\cdots,\vec{v_n})\) be a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\).
Then the \(\mathfrak{B}\)-matrix B of T is diagonal if and only if \(T(\vec{v_1})=c_1 \vec{v_1},\cdots,T(\vec{v_n})=c_n \vec{v_n}\) for some scalars \(c_1,\cdots, c_n\).
From a geometrical point of view, this means that \(T(\vec{v_j})\) is parallel to \(\vec{v_j}\) for all \(j=1,\cdots,n\).
