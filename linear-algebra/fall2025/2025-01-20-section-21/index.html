2.1 Linear Transformations and their Inverses

MATH 3330 · Dr. Esteban Diaz
Definition 2.1.1
Linear Transformations
A function T from $\mathbb{R}^m$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$ is called a linear transformation there exists an $n \times m$ matrix A such that: $$T(\vec{x}) = A\vec{x}$$ for all $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$.
Example 5
Consider the letter L in the plane, made up of the vectors $\vec{v}_1 = (1, 0)$ and $\vec{v}_2 = (0, 1)$. The Geogebra applet shows the effect of the linear transformation $T:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$ defined by $$T(\vec{x})=\begin{pmatrix} cos(\theta) & -sin(\theta) \\ sin(\theta) & cos(\theta) \end{pmatrix}\vec{x}$$ for various values of $\theta$ in $[0,2\pi]$.
Example 6
Consider the linear transformation $T(\vec{x}) = A\vec{x}$ where $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{pmatrix}$.
Find $T(\vec{v})$ where $\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $T(\vec{w})$ where $\vec{w} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
Theorem 2.1.2
The columns of the matrix of a linear transformation
Consider a linear transformation $T:\mathbb{R}^m\to\mathbb{R}^n$. Then, the matrix of $T$ is: $$A = \begin{pmatrix} T(\vec{e}_1) & T(\vec{e}_2) & \cdots & T(\vec{e}_m) \end{pmatrix}$$ where $\vec{e}_1, \vec{e}_2, \ldots, \vec{e}_m$ are the standard basis vectors for $\mathbb{R}^m$.
To justify this result use theorem 1.3.8.
Theorem 2.1.3
Linear transformations
A transformation $T$ from $\mathbb{R}^m$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$ is linear if and only if:
  • $T(\vec{v} + \vec{w}) = T(\vec{v}) + T(\vec{w})$ for all $\vec{v}, \vec{w}$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$
  • $T(k\vec{v}) = kT(\vec{v})$ for all $\vec{v}$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$ and all scalars $k$
