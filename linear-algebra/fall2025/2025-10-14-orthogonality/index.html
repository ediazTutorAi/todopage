Orthogonality

MATH 3330 · Dr. Esteban Diaz
Definition 5.1.1
Orthogonality, length, unit vectors
a. Two vectors $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ are orthogonal if their dot product is zero, that is, if $\vec{u}\cdot \vec{v}=0$. We write $\vec{u}\perp \vec{v}$ to indicate that $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ are orthogonal.

b. The length or norm of a vector $\vec{v}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ is the non-negative number $\|\vec{v}\|=\sqrt{\vec{v}\cdot \vec{v}}=\sqrt{v_1^2+\cdots+v_n^2}$.

c. A vector $\vec{u}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ is a unit vector if its length is 1, that is, if $\|\vec{u}\|=1$.
Definition 5.1.2
Orthonormal vectors
A set of vectors $\{\vec{u_1},\cdots,\vec{u_m}\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ is orthonormal if the vectors are unit vectors and pairwise orthogonal, that is, if $\|\vec{u_i}\|=1$ for all i from 1 to m, and if $\vec{u_i}\cdot \vec{u_j}=0$ whenever $i\ne j$.
Theorem 5.1.3
Properties of orthonormal vectors
Let $\{\vec{u_1},\cdots,\vec{u_m}\}$ be an orthonormal set of vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Then:

a. The set $\{\vec{u_1},\cdots,\vec{u_m}\}$ is linearly independent.

b. Orthonormal vectors form a basis of their span. In particular, if m=n, then $\{\vec{u_1},\cdots,\vec{u_m}\}$ is a basis of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.

Theorem 5.1.4
Orthogonal Projection
Consider a vector $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ and a subspace V of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Then we can write $$\vec{x}=\vec{x}^{||}+\vec{x}^{\perp}$$ where $\vec{x}^{||}$ is in V and $\vec{x}^{\perp}$ is perpendicular to V, and this representation is unique. The vector $\vec{x}^{||}$ is called the orthogonal projection of $\vec{x}$ onto V, denoted by $proj_V(\vec{x})$. The transformation $proj_V:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to V$ is a linear transformation.
Theorem 5.1.5
Formula for the orthogonal projection
If V is subspace of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ with an orthonormal basis $\{\vec{u_1},\cdots,\vec{u_m}\}$, then for any vector $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$, the orthogonal projection of $\vec{x}$ onto V is given by $$proj_V(\vec{x})=(\vec{x}\cdot \vec{u_1})\vec{u_1}+\cdots+(\vec{x}\cdot \vec{u_m})\vec{u_m}$$ for all $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.
Theorem 5.1.6
Consider an orthonormal basis $\{\vec{u_1},\cdots,\vec{u_n}\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Then any vector $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ can be expressed as $$\vec{x}=(\vec{x}\cdot \vec{u_1})\vec{u_1}+\cdots+(\vec{x}\cdot \vec{u_n})\vec{u_n}$$ for all $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.
Definition 5.1.7
Orthogonal Complement
Consider a subspace V of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. The orthogonal complement of V, denoted by $V^{\perp}$, is the set of all vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ that are orthogonal to every vector in V, that is, $$V^{\perp}=\{\vec{x}\in \mathbb{R}^{n}:\vec{x}\cdot \vec{v}=0\text{ for all }\vec{v}\in V\}$$ Note that $V^{\perp}$ is the kernel of the orthogonal projection $proj_V:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to V$.
Theorem 5.1.8
Properties of the orthogonal complement
Consider a subspace V of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Then:

a. The orthogonal complement $V^{\perp}$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.

b. The intersection of V and $V^{\perp}$ is $\{\vec{0}\}$.

c. The dimensions of V and $V^{\perp}$ add up to n, that is, $dim(V)+dim(V^{\perp})=n$.

d. The orthogonal complement of $V^{\perp}$ is V, that is, $(V^{\perp})^{\perp}=V$.
Theorem 5.1.9
Pythagorean Theorem
Consider two vectors $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. The equation $\|\vec{u}+\vec{v}\|^2=\|\vec{u}\|^2+\|\vec{v}\|^2$ holds if and only if $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ are orthogonal.
Theorem 5.1.10
An inequality for the magnitude of the projection
Consider a subspace V of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ and a vector $\vec{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Then $\|proj_V(\vec{x})\|\le \|\vec{x}\|$, with equality if and only if $\vec{x}$ is in V.
Cauchy-Schwarz
inequality
If $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ are vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$, then $$|\vec{u}\cdot \vec{v}|\le \|\vec{u}\|\|\vec{v}\|$$ with equality if and only if $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ are parallel.
Definition 5.1.12
Angle between two vectors
Consider two non-zero vectors $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. The angle $\theta$ between $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ is defined by $$\cos(\theta)=\frac{\vec{u}\cdot \vec{v}}{\|\vec{u}\|\|\vec{v}\|}$$ where $0\le \theta \le \pi$.
