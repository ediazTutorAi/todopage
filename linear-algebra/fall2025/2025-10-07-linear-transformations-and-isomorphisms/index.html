Linear transformations and Isomorphisms

MATH 3330 · Dr. Esteban Diaz
Definition 4.2.1
Linear transformations, image, kernel, rank, nullity
Consider two linear spaces V and W. A function T from V to W is called a linear transformation if
$$T(f+g)=T(f)+T(g)\text{ and } T(kf)=kT(f)$$ for all elements $f \text{ and } g$ of V and for all scalars k. These two rules are referred to as the sum rule and the constant-multiple rule, respectively.
For a linear transformation T from V to W, we let
$$im(T)=\{T(f):f \in V\}$$ and $$ker(T)=\{f \in V:T(f)=0\}$$ Note that $im(T)$ is a subspace of targe space W and the \(ker(T)\) is a subspace of domain V.
Linear transformations, image, kernel, rank, nullity
continued
If the image of T is finite dimensional, then $dim(imT)$ is called the rank of T, and if the kernel of T is finite dimensional, then dim(kerT) is the nullity of T.
If V is finite dimensional, then the rank-nullity theorem holds.
$$dim(V)=rank(T)+nullity(T)=dim(im(T))+dim(ker(T))$$
Examples
  • $D(f)=f'$ from $C^{\infty}$ to $C^{\infty}$
  • $I(f)=\int_0^1 f(x)dx$ from $C[0,1]$ to $\mathbb{R}$
  • $T(x_0,x_1,x_2,\cdots)=(x_1,x_2,x_3,\cdots)$
  • $$L\left(\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\right)=\begin{bmatrix} a\\ b\\ c\\ d \end{bmatrix}$$ from $\mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{4}$
Observe that L is an isomorphism from $\mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{4}$
Definition 4.2.2
Isomorphisms and isomorphic spaces
An invertible linear transformation T is called an isomorphism. We say that the linear space V is isomorphic to the linear space W if there exists an isomorphism T from V to W.
Theorem 4.2.3
Coordinate transformations are isomorphims
If $\mathfrak{B}=(f_1,f_2,\cdots,f_n)$ is a basis of a linear space V, then the coordinate transformation $L_{\mathfrak{B}}(f)=[f]_{\mathfrak{B}}$ from V to $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ is an isomorphism. Thus, V is isomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^{n}$; the linear spaces V and $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ have the same structure.
Definition 4.1.3
Span, linear independence, basis, coordinates
Consider the elements \(f_1,\cdots,f_n\) in a linear space V.
  • a. We say that \(f_1,\cdots,f_n\) span V if every \(f\) in V can be expressed as a linear combination of \(f_1,\cdots,f_n\)
  • b. We say \(f_i\) is redundant if it is a linear combination of \(f_1,\cdots,f_{i-1}\). The elements \(f_1,\cdots,f_n\) are called linearly independent if none of them is redundant. This is the case if the equation
    $$c_1f_1+\cdots+c_nf_n=0$$ has only the trivial solution $$c_1=\cdots=c_n=0$$
Span, linear independence, basis, coordinates (continued)
We say that elements \(f_1,\cdots,f_n\) are a basis of V is they span V and are linearly independent. This means that every f in V can be written uniquely as a linear combination $f=c_1f_1+\cdots+c_nf_n$. The coefficients \(c_1,\cdots,c_n\) are called the coordinates of f with respect to the basis \(\mathfrak{B}=(f_1,\cdots,f_n)\). The vector $$\begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{bmatrix}$$ in \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\) is called the \(\mathfrak{B}\)-coordinate vector of f, denoted by \([f]_{\mathfrak{B}}\)
The transformation $$L(f)=[f]_\mathfrak{B}=\begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{bmatrix} $$ from V to \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\) is called the \(\mathfrak{B}\)-coordinate transformation, sometimes denoted by \(L_{\mathfrak{B}}\)
Theorem 4.1.4
Linearity of the coordinate transformation \(L_{\mathfrak{B}}\)
If \(\mathfrak{B}\) is a basis of a linear space V, then:
  • \([f+g]_{\mathfrak{B}}=[f]_{\mathfrak{B}}+[g]_{\mathfrak{B}}\) for all elements f and g of V, and
  • \([kf]_{\mathfrak{B}}=k[f]_{\mathfrak{B}}\) for all f and V and for all scalars k
Theorem 4.1.5
Dimension
If a linear space V has a basis with n elements, then all other bases of V consist of n elements as well. We say that n is dimension of V: $$dim(V)=n$$
Examples from Dimension
1. Find a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}\).
2. Find a basis of \(P_2\) the space of all polynomials of degree \(\leq 2\), and thus determine the dimension of \(P_2\).
Summary 4.1.6
Finding a basis of a linear space V
a. Write down a typical element of V, in terms of some arbitrary constants.
b. Using the arbitrary constants as coefficients, express your typical elements as a linear combination of some elements of V.
c. Verify that the elements of V in this linear combination are linearly independent, then they will form a basis of V.
Examples
Basis
Find a basis of the space V of all matrices B that commute with \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\)
Theorem 4.1.7
Linear differential equations
The solutions of the differential equation. $$f''(x)+af'(x)+bf(x)=0$$ where a, b are constants, form a two-dimensional subspace of the space \(C^{\infty}\) of smooth functions. More generally, the solutions of the differential equation $$f^{(n)}(x)+a_{n-1}f^{(n-1)}+\cdots+a_1f'(x)+a_0f(x)=0$$ where \(a_0,\cdots,a_{n-1}\) are constants form an n-dimensional subspace of \(C^{\infty}\). A differential equation of this form is called an nth-order differential equation with constant coefficients.
Definition 4.1.8
Finite dimensional linear spaces
A linear space V is called finite dimensional if it has a (finite) basis \(f_1,\cdots,f_n\). Otherwise, the space is called infinite dimensional. These ideas are from 1888.
