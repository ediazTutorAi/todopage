3.2 Subspaces, Bases and Linear Independence

MATH 3330 · Dr. Esteban Diaz
Definition 3.2.1
Subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$
A subset W of the vector space $\mathbb{R}^n$ is called a (linear) subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ if it has the following three properties:
  • W contains the zero vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$
  • W is closed under addition: If $\vec{w}_1, \vec{w}_2 \in W$, then $\vec{w}_1 + \vec{w}_2 \in W$
  • W is closed under scalar multiplication: If $\vec{w} \in W$ and $k \in \mathbb{R}$, then $k\vec{w} \in W$
Properties (2) and (3) together mean that W is closed under linear combinations.
Theorem 3.2.2
Image and kernel are subspaces
If $T(\vec{x})=A\vec{x}$ is a linear transformation from $\mathbb{R}^m$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$, then:
  • $\text{Im}(T)$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$
  • $\text{Ker}(T)$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$
Definition 3.2.3
Redundant vectors; linear independence; basis
Consider vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_k$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
a. We say that a vector $\vec{v}_i$ in the list $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ is redundatn if $\vec{v}_i$ is a linear combination of the preceding vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_{i-1}$.
b. The vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ are called linearly independent if none of them is redundant. Otherwise, the vectors are called linearly dependent (meaning at least one of them is redundant)
c. We say that the vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ in a subspace $V$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ form a basis for $V$ if they span $V$ and are linearly independent.
Theorem 3.2.4
Basis of the image
To construct a basis of the image of a matrix A, list all the column vectors of A, and omit the redundant vectors from this list.

But how can we identify the redundant vectors?
Theorem 3.2.5
Linear independence and zero components
Consider vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. If $\vec{v}_1$ is nonzero, and if each of the vectors $\vec{v}_i$ for $i \geq 2$ has a nonzero entry in a component where all preceding vectors $\vec{v}_1,\ldots,\vec{v}_{i-1}$ have a 0, then the vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ are linearly independent.
Definition 3.2.6
Linear Relations
Consider the vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. An equation of the form $$c_1\vec{v}_1 + c_2\vec{v}_2 + \cdots + c_m\vec{v}_m = \vec{0}$$ where $c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_m$ are scalars, is called a linear relation among the vectors. There is always the trivial relation, with $c_1 = c_2 = \cdots = c_m = 0$. Nontrivial relations (where at least one coefficient $c_i$ is nonzero) may or many not exist among the vectors $\vec{v}_1,\ldots,\vec{v}_m$
Theorem 3.2.7
Relations and linear dependence
The vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ are linearly dependent if and only if there are nontrivial linear relations among them.
Theorem 3.2.8
Kernel and relations
The vectors in the kernel of an $n \times m$ matrix $A$ correspond to the linear relations among the column vectors of $A$: The equation $$A\vec{x} = \vec{0}$$ means that $$x_1\vec{v}_1 + x_2\vec{v}_2 + \cdots + x_m\vec{v}_m = \vec{0}$$ In particular, the column vectors of A are linearly independent if and only if $\text{Ker}(A) = \{\vec{0}\}$, or, equivalently, if $\text{rank}(A) = m$. This condition implies that $m\leq n$. Thus, we can find at most n linearly independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
Summary 3.2.9
Various characterizations of linear independence
For a list $\vec{v}_1, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ of vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the following statements are equivalent:
  • The vectors are linearly independent.
  • None of the vectos is redundant, meaning that none of them is linear combination of the preceding vectors
  • None of the vectors is a linear combination of the other vectors in the list
  • The only linear relation among the vectors is the trivial relation
  • The kernel of the matrix $A$ whose columns are the vectors $\vec{v}_1, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ is $\{\vec{0}\}$.
  • The rank of the matrix $A$ whose columns are the vectors $\vec{v}_1, \ldots, \vec{v}_m$ is equal to $m$.
