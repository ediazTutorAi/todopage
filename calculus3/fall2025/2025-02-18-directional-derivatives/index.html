Directional Derivatives

MATH 2326 · Dr. Esteban Diaz
Definition
Directional Derivative
Suppose $z=f(x,y)$ is a function of two variables with a domain of D. Let $(a,b)$ be a point in D and define $\vec{u}=cos(\theta)\hat{i}+sin(\theta)\hat{j}$. Then, the directional derivative of f in the direction of $\vec{u}$ is given by $$D_{\vec{u}}f(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+hcos(\theta), b+hsin(\theta))-f(a,b)}{h}$$ provided the limit exists.
Theorem 4.12
Directional Derivative of a Function of Two Variables
Let $z=f(x,y)$ be a function of two variables x and y, and assume that $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist and $f(x,y)$ is differentiable everywhere. Then the directional derivative of $f$ in the direction of $\vec{u}=cos(\theta)\hat{i}+sin(\theta)\hat{j}$ is given by $$ D_{\vec{u}}f(x,y) = f_x(x,y)cos(\theta) + f_y(x,y)sin(\theta) $$
Gradient
Definition
Let $z=f(x,y)$ be a function of x and y such that $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist. The vector $\nabla f(x,y) = \langle f_x(x,y), f_y(x,y) \rangle$ is called the gradient of f. The directional derivative of f in the direction of $\vec{u}$ is given by $$D_{\vec{u}}f(x,y) = \nabla f(x,y) \cdot \vec{u}$$
Theorem 4.13
Properties of the Gradient
Suppose the function $z=f(x,y)$ is differentiable at $(x_0,y_0)$. Then:
  1. If $\nabla f(x_0,y_0)=\vec{0}$, then $D_\vec{u}f(x_0,y_0)=0$ for all unit vectors $\vec{u}$.
  2. If $\nabla f(x_0,y_0) \neq \vec{0}$, then $D_{\vec{u}}f(x_0,y_0)$ has a maximum value of $\left|\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\right|$ in the direction of $\nabla f(x_0,y_0)$ and a minimum value of $-\left|\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\right|$ in the direction of $-\nabla f(x_0,y_0)$.
Gradients and Level Curves
Gradient is normal to the level curves
Suppose the function $z=f(x,y)$ has continuous first-order partial derivatives in an open disk centered at the point $(x_0,y_0)$. If $\nabla f(x_0,y_0) \neq \vec{0}$, then $\nabla f(x_0,y_0)$ is normal to the level curve of f at $(x_0,y_0)$.

Tangent Planes
Definition
Let $P_0=(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ be a point on a surface S, and let C be any curve passing through $P_0$ and lying entirely ins S. If the tangent lines to all such curves C at $P_0$ lie in the same plane, then this plane is called the tangent plane to S at $P_0$.
Definition
Let S be a surface defined by a differential function $z=f(x,y)$, and let $P_0=(x_0,y_0)$ be a point in the domain of f. Then, the equation of the tangent plane to S at $P_0$ is given by $$z =f(x_0,y_0) + f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0) + f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0)$$ Observe the relation between this is the gradient we studied before.
