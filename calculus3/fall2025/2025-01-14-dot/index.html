Vectors in the Plane and in Space

2.1 Vectors Page 1
Definition and Notation
A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. A vector is represented by an arrow. The length of the arrow represents the magnitude of the vector. The direction of the arrow represents the direction of the vector. The endpoints of the segment are called the initial point and the terminal point. The length of the segment represent the magnitude of the vector. The magnitude is represented by double vertical bars, like $||\vec{v}||$
2.1 Vectors Page 2
Representation, Zero Vector, Equivalent Vectors
A vector in the plane is represented by a directed line segment (arrow) from an initial point to a terminal point. A vector whose initial and terminal points coincide is the zero vector (no direction). Two vectors are equivalent if they have the same magnitude and the same direction, we treat them as equal.

2.1 Vectors Page 4
Component Form (Definition) + Rule from Two Points
A standard-position vector with terminal point $(x,y)$ is written in component form as $\langle x, y\rangle$. If $\overrightarrow{PQ}$ has initial point $P(x_1,y_1)$ and terminal point $Q(x_2,y_2)$, then $$\overrightarrow{PQ}=\langle x_2-x_1,\; y_2-y_1\rangle$$
2.1 Vectors Page 5
Vector Operations in Components
If $\vec v=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle$ and $\vec w=\langle w_1,w_2\rangle$ and $k$ is a scalar, then $$k\vec v=\langle kv_1,kv_2\rangle,\qquad \vec v+\vec w=\langle v_1+w_1,\; v_2+w_2\rangle.$$ You can also see that: $$\vec{v}-\vec{w}=\vec{v}+\(-\vec{w}\)$$
2.1 Vectors Page 6
Magnitude Formula
If $\vec v=\langle x,y\rangle$, then $$||\vec v||=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}.$$ This is just an application of the Pythagorean theorem.
2.1 Vectors Page 7
Magnitude + Direction ⇒ Components (Trig)
If $||\vec v||=r$ and $\theta$ is the direction angle from the positive $x$-axis, then $$\vec v=\langle r\cos\theta,\; r\sin\theta\rangle.$$
2.1 Vectors Page 8
Unit Vectors, Normalization, and $\mathbf{i},\mathbf{j}$
A unit vector has magnitude $1$. For any nonzero $\vec v$, the unit vector in its direction is $$\hat u=\frac{1}{||\vec v||}\vec v$$ The standard unit vectors are $\mathbf{i}=\langle 1,0\rangle$ and $\mathbf{j}=\langle 0,1\rangle$, and any vector $\langle x,y\rangle$ can be written as $x\mathbf{i}+y\mathbf{j}$.
2.1 Vectors Page 9
Theorem 2.1: Properties of Vector Operations
For vectors $\vec u,\vec v,\vec w$ and scalars $a,b$ (in the plane):
  • $\vec u+\vec v=\vec v+\vec u$ (commutative)
  • $(\vec u+\vec v)+\vec w=\vec u+(\vec v+\vec w)$ (associative)
  • $\vec v+\vec 0=\vec v$ (additive identity)
  • $\vec v+(-\vec v)=\vec 0$ (additive inverse)
  • $a(\vec u+\vec v)=a\vec u+a\vec v$ (distributive)
  • $(a+b)\vec v=a\vec v+b\vec v$ (distributive)
  • $a(b\vec v)=(ab)\vec v$, and $1\vec v=\vec v$
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 10
The 3D Coordinate System
Points in space are written as $(x,y,z)$ using three mutually perpendicular axes: $x$, $y$, and $z$. The coordinate planes are the $xy$-, $xz$-, and $yz$-planes.
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 11
Vectors and Component Form
A vector in space is represented by a directed line segment. A standard-position vector with terminal point $(x,y,z)$ is $$\vec v=\langle x,y,z\rangle.$$ If $P(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and $Q(x_2,y_2,z_2)$, then $$\overrightarrow{PQ}=\langle x_2-x_1,\;y_2-y_1,\;z_2-z_1\rangle.$$
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 12
Vector Operations
If $\vec v=\langle v_1,v_2,v_3\rangle$, $\vec w=\langle w_1,w_2,w_3\rangle$, and $k$ is a scalar: $$\vec v+\vec w=\langle v_1+w_1,\;v_2+w_2,\;v_3+w_3\rangle,$$ $$k\vec v=\langle kv_1,kv_2,kv_3\rangle.$$
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 13
Magnitude (Key Formula)
If $\vec v=\langle x,y,z\rangle$, then $$||\vec v||=\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}.$$ (Distance formula in space.)
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 14
Unit Vectors and Normalization
A unit vector has magnitude $1$. For $\vec v\neq\vec 0$, the unit vector in its direction is $$\hat u=\frac{1}{||\vec v||}\vec v.$$
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 15
Standard Basis Vectors
The standard unit vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ are $$\mathbf{i}=\langle 1,0,0\rangle,\quad \mathbf{j}=\langle 0,1,0\rangle,\quad \mathbf{k}=\langle 0,0,1\rangle.$$ Any vector can be written as $$\vec v=x\mathbf{i}+y\mathbf{j}+z\mathbf{k}.$$
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 16
Direction Angles
If $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are the angles a vector makes with the $x$-, $y$-, and $z$-axes, then $$\cos\alpha=\frac{x}{||\vec v||},\quad \cos\beta=\frac{y}{||\vec v||},\quad \cos\gamma=\frac{z}{||\vec v||}.$$ These are called the direction cosines.
2.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions Page 17
Parallel Vectors
Two nonzero vectors are parallel if one is a scalar multiple of the other: $$\vec v \parallel \vec w \iff \vec v = k\vec w.$$
