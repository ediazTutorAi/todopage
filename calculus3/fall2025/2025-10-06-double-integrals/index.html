5.1 Double Integration over Rectangular Regions
Definition
The double integral of the function $f(x,y)$ over the rectangular region R in the xy-plane is defined $$\iint_{R} f(x,y) \, dV \, dA=\lim_{m,n \to \infty}\sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n f(x_i^*,y_j^*)\delta A $$ But what do all of these mean?
Rectangular Division
Observe that our domain is a rectangle in the xy-plane. We have divided this rectangle into several smaller sub-rectangles. For each of these sub-rectangles, we select a representative point, which we’ll denote by $(x_j, y_j)$. At that point, we evaluate our function $f(x, y)$. The value $f(x_j, y_j)$ gives us the height of a rectangular “box” built above that small region.
By multiplying this height $f(x_j, y_j)$ by the area of the base sub-rectangle, we obtain the volume of that small box.
If we repeat this process for every sub-rectangle and then add up all those small volumes, we get an approximation for the total volume under the surface $z = f(x, y)$ and above the rectangular region in the plane.
As we make the subdivisions finer and finer, this approximation becomes more accurate and in the limit, it becomes the double integral of $f(x, y)$ over the region.
Double Integral
Visualization in 3D
In this GeoGebra visualization, you can see how each of the small boxes is formed above the sub-rectangles of our domain. This picture helps us visualize how the double integral is constructed, by summing up the volumes of all these tiny boxes.
You can think of the double integral as representing the total volume under the surface $z = f(x, y)$. Notice, however, that this interpretation works directly only when the surface lies above the xy-plane, since in that case the function values are positive and the volume makes sense as a positive quantity.
Double Integral
for General Regions
In this GeoGebra visualization, we are looking at a more general region, which we have divided into rectangular strips. (We are not showing the smaller subdivisions this time.)
Notice that, to cover the entire region, these strips extend from the left to the right across the x-values of the region. The height of each strip goes from the bottom curve up to the top curve.
As we imagine taking finer and finer strips, their width becomes smaller and smaller — approaching zero. However, since the width never actually reaches zero, we represent it symbolically as dx.
