Power Series
A series of the form \[\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_nx^n=c_0+c_1x+c_2x^2+\cdots\]is a power series centered at \(x=0\). A series of the form \[\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_n(x-a)^n=c_0+c_1(x-a)+c_2(x-a)^2+\cdots\]is a power series centered at \(x=a\). We are defining that \(x^0=1\) and \((x-a)^0=1\) even when x=0 and x=a respectively.
Theorem 6.1
Convergence of a Power Series
Consider the power series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_n(x-a)^n\). The series satisfies exactly one of the following properties:
  1. The series converges at \(x=a\) and diverges for all \(x\neq a\).
  2. The series converges for all real numbers x.
  3. There exists a real number R > 0 such that the series converges if \(|x-a| < R\) and diverges if \(|x-a| > R\). At the values x where \(|x-a|=R\), the series may converge or diverge.
Interval of convergence, Radius of convergence
Consider the power series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_n(x-a)^n\). The set of real numbers x where the series converges is the interval of convergence. If there exists a real number R > 0 such that the series converges for \(|x-a| < R\) and diverges for \(|x-a| > R\), then R is the radius of convergence. If the series converges only at x=a, we say the radius of converges is R = 0. If the series converges for all real numbers x, we say the radius of convergence is \(R = \infty\).
Example 1

Solve \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^n}{n!}\) Use ratio Test\(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}}{\frac{x^n}{n!}}\right|\)

\(=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{n!x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!x^n}\right|\)\(=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{x}{n+1}\right|\)

\(=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{|x|}{n+1}\)

Then for all x a real number we have \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{|x|}{n+1}=0\)

Then the interval of radiues of convergence is \(R=\infty\) and the interval of converge is all the real numbers

Example 2

Find the interval and radius of convergence of \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n!x^n\). What test?

Use the ratio test: \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)!x^{n+1}}{n!x^n}\right|\). This simplifies to:

\(=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}|(n+1)x|\)

This limit is infinity for all the values of x. Therefore, the radius of convergence is 0 and the interval of convergence is just the point 0

Example 3

Find the interval and the radius of convergence of the series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(x-2)^n}{(n+1)3^n}\)

\(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{(x-2)^{n+1}}{((n+1)+1)3^{n+1}}}{\frac{(x-2)^n}{(n+1)3^n}}\right|\)\(=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(x-2)^{n+1}(n+1)3^n}{(x-2)^n(n+2)3^{n+1}}\right|\)\(=\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(x-2)(n+1)}{(n+2)3}\right|\)

\(=\displaystyle\left|\frac{x-2}{3}\right|\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n+1}{n+2}\)\(=\left|\frac{x-2}{3}\right|=\rho\)

Remember that in order for this series to converge \(\rho\) must be < 1 also remember that when \(\rho=1\) the test is inconclusive so we need another test to show convergence or divergence

\(\left|\frac{x-2}{3}\right|\lt 1\) implies \(\left|x-2\right|\lt3\). This means the radius of convergence is 3 centered at \(x=2\). This means the interval of convergence so far is \((-1,5)\). We need to analize the ends \(x=-1\) and \(x=5\)

Case \(x=1\). \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{(n+1)}\) This is an alternate series, whih is the same as the Alternating Harmonic Series, so it converges

Case \(x=5\)

The series is \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(n+1)}\), which is the harmonic series, divergent

Therefore, the interval of convergence is \([-1,5)\)
